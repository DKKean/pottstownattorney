DUI penalties in Pennsylvania (informational)

The information below is organized from public reference materials. Penalties are fact-specific, and the law can change. Always verify against your paperwork and current law.

Not legal advice. Information on this site is for general education only. Consult an attorney. Official driver and vehicle information: Pennsylvania PennDOT.

Tiers in PA materials (general map)

General

Commonly used for a lower end of the stated range in some first-offense overviews. Still a criminal case, not a “minor” ticket in every sense.

Higher stated BAC / elevated exposure

High

Escalated ranges in official summaries; can move license, jail, and cost bands compared with a general first tier.

Heaviest “standard tier” in many tables

Highest

Top impairment tier in typical charts or some drug-influence allegations. Compare your year of offense and the complaint.

General impairment (.08 to .099% BAC), no prior offenses

  • Ungraded misdemeanor
  • Up to six months probation
  • $300 fine
  • Alcohol Highway Safety School
  • Treatment when ordered

High BAC (.10 to .159% BAC), no prior offenses

  • Ungraded misdemeanor
  • Twelve-month license suspension
  • Forty-eight hours to six months in prison
  • $500–$5,000 fine
  • Alcohol Highway Safety School, treatment when ordered

Highest BAC (.16% or higher) or controlled substance, no prior offenses

  • Ungraded misdemeanor
  • Twelve-month license suspension
  • Seventy-two hours to six months in prison
  • $1,000–$5,000 fine
  • Alcohol Highway Safety School, treatment when ordered

Repeat offenses and higher tiers

Prior offenses, higher BAC, and other facts can increase jail exposure, license loss, fines, and ignition interlock. The complete framework is case-specific. Call the office with your charging documents.

Serious felony-grade allegations (illustrative)

Some patterns (such as a fourth offense within a ten-year period, or certain homicide-by-vehicle matters) are discussed in the highest severity ranges in Pennsylvania law materials. Those sections must be read against the statute in effect for your year of offense.

Ignition interlock, plates, and license administration

Case-specific review required. Interlock, restoration, and plate rules follow your offense year, priors, and what was actually ordered. Do not treat a web chart as a substitute for your documents.

Many DUI dispositions and some suspensions connect to administrative requirements that sit alongside court dates. Ignition interlock, for example, is a device-based condition that is described in state driver-law materials; who must install it, and for how long, depends on the offense tier, prior record, and other factors. License restoration is not always automatic; treat any PennDOT notice as a deadline-bearing document, not background noise.

If you are comparing online penalty charts, also read the DUI defense overview and bring your actual paperwork to a consultation; charts cannot account for your specific charges, years of offense, or local practice.
