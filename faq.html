Frequently asked questions

Straight answers, organized by theme. This is not a substitute for legal advice about your case.

Why do I need a lawyer for my DUI case?

Asking a friend for legal advice or using the internet is not a substitute for counsel with years of experience. You need an attorney who knows Pennsylvania law and can protect your rights. If you are not sure, schedule a consultation for a case-specific review.

I only had a few drinks and did not feel drunk. How can I be charged?

In Pennsylvania, you can be charged with a DUI if your blood alcohol content (BAC) is at or above the legal limit for the charge. You can be at or over the limit even if you do not “feel” impaired.

How many drinks does it take to reach the legal limit?

That number varies with weight, timing, food, and other variables. Charts are rough reference only. Each drink can bring a driver closer to the limit—your case depends on the testing and the facts, not a generic calculator.

I was not drinking. Why was I still charged with DUI?

Pennsylvania law can address impaired driving from alcohol, drugs, or a combination. If impairment by a substance is alleged, the case may proceed as a DUI. Discuss the specific allegations with an attorney.

Can I refuse the breath test?

Pennsylvania is an implied-consent state. Refusals and testing decisions can have serious license consequences and can affect the criminal case. The right call depends on the exact process used and the timing. This is a topic to take up with legal counsel as early as possible.

What are the different levels of DUI?

There are three common BAC tiers (general impairment, high, highest) and additional issues when drugs or a refusal is involved. The tier helps drive the grading and the penalty range. See the DUI Penalties page and official PennDOT materials.

Can I choose which chemical test to take?

What you are offered, in what order, and how that interacts with implied-consent and license rules is case-specific. Do not guess from a general page; call the office to walk through the testing sequence and paperwork in your file.

When an officer has me follow a penlight, what is that test?

That is often the horizontal gaze nystagmus (HGN) test, which is one of several field evaluations. Whether it is used effectively against you can depend on administration, training, and the full record. Your attorney can evaluate how the test fits your defense.

What are the penalties for a DUI?

Penalties depend on prior offenses, the tier, and the facts. Start with the DUI Penalties overview and the PA DMV / PennDOT information for the year of your offense, then get advice from counsel.

I keep hearing the letters “ARD.” What does that mean?

ARD generally refers to a diversion-style program offered in some Pennsylvania cases when the law, the facts, and the prosecutor’s policies allow. It is not available to everyone, and it is not a “right.” Whether it fits your case — and what you must do to complete it — must be read against the governing rules in your county and the statute in effect. Ask counsel after reviewing your charging document; do not assume you qualify because someone online said so.
